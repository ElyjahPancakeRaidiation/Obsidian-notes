**Ionization energy** (symbol IE) generally increases as we go across or up the Periodic Table. Ionization energy is the energy required to pull an electron away from the attractive **electric force** of the nucleus. The stronger the force, the more energy is required. 

Two factors can determine the strength of an electric force:
- The magnitude of the electric charge. Charges with larger magnitudes create stronger electric forces. The larger the effective nuclear charge $Z^*$, the stronger the attractive force the nucleus exerts, and the higher ionization energy. $Z^*$ generally increases going across the Periodic table, from left to right, IE also generally increases going across the Periodic table.
- The distance between charges. Greater separation reduces the strength of the electric force between charges. The average distance of outer electrons from the nucleus grows as we move down the Periodic Table, because there are more inner electron shells. This tends to lower the ionization energy.
- Each electron you take costs more than the last since your getting closer to nucleus. Also depends if you take a core electron it will cost even more energy.

## Electron Affinity
- The electron affinity is the negative of deltaH for the process where an atom or anion in the gas phase gains an electron.
- Thee first EA for an atom is usually [exothermic](obsidian://open?vault=Chemistry&file=Chemistry%201134%20missed%20notes). The incoming electron feels more attraction to the incompletely shielded nucleus. However, the second EA is [endothermic](obsidian://open?vault=Chemistry&file=Chemistry%201134%20missed%20notes). The negative electron is entering a negative ion.
