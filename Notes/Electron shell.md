An atom is made of three shells and each shell can hold a certain amount of electrons. The first shell around the atom can hold up to 2, the second shell can hold up to 8, and the third shell can hold up to 8 or more depending on the atom.

The outermost shell is called the valence shell where the valence electrons are stored.
## Valence Electrons
- Valence electrons are electrons that can form [[Chemical bonds]]. They also affect the behavior of the atom.
- Valence electrons can be found with the periodic table. Their **group number** correlates with their outermost shell. If the group number is a double digit just look at the last number.
- **TEACHER DEFINTION**: Electrons that are not in unfilled shells are called valence electrons and are the ones able to be reacted.
- **Helium** is an exception though with it only having 2 valence electrons.
- Valence electrons change the atoms behavior due to its need to complete its most outer shell. If the atom only has 1 or 2 valence electrons it wants to get rid of those 2 as soon as possible. However, anymore and it instead wants to complete the most outer shell (Helium is the exception).
	- A definition for this is the **Reactivity** of the atom. How easily an element combines with other elements to form new substances (Noble gasses don't react at all).
	- Atoms with either 2, 1, 6, or 7, electrons are highly reactive while 8 are stable. 3, 4, or 5 valence electrons tend to react differently and do not have a high reactivity.
- When dealing with valence electrons in molecules its very simple to find the amount.
	- Example: $S O^(()2 -)_(4)$
	- Sulfur is in group 16 so it contains 6 valence electrons while Oxygen is also in group 16 so it contains 6 valence electrons as well.
	- multiply the valence electrons with the amount of moles the atom contains.
	- S: 1 * 6 = 6
	- $O_4$: 4 * 6 = 24
	- In total is 30 valence electrons. However, if a molecule has a charge we need to add or subtract the amount by that charge. If it is a positive charge we subtract and a negative charge we add. So the total valence electron of $S O^(()2 -)_(4)$ is 32 valence electrons.

## Electron structure
- **Principial quantum number**, usually given the symbol *n*, is one of the four quantum numbers that identify a state of an electron in an atom.
	- Must be an integer greater than 0.
	- The value *n* tells us something about the distance from the nucleus at which you're likely to find the electron. Electrons in states with higher *n* are more likely to be found farther from the nucleus. Basically what we said about the shells before, an electron in a state with n = 1 is in the atoms 1st shell.
	- It also tells you a little about energy of the electron since energy depends on the distance between charges. Electrons in states with a higher n are most likely have higher energy than electrons in states with lower n. The **ground state** of an electron is always the smallest possible value of *n*.
- **Angular momentum quantum number**, usually give the symbol *I*, Identifies the approximate angular momentum of an electron. Electrons in states with higher *I* have higher angular momentum.
	- must be an integer greater than or equal to 0.
	- For the given state of an electron in an atom, *I* must always be less than *n*.
	- The angular momentum about a point (in this case the nucleus) is a combination of orbital velocity and distance from the point, this means electrons in states with higher *I* are orbiting the nucleus faster, further out, or both. Conversely, electrons in states with lower *I* are orbiting the nucleus slower, closer in, or both. 
	- Electrons with different angular momentums also affect how they are distributed around the atom. Or the value of *I* determines the shape of the orbital state.
	- The **ground state** of an electron is always the smallest possible value of *I*.
	- Although energy is mostly determined by the principal quantum number, the value *I* still has some influence on the energy of an electron in the state.
		- Chemist usually use a letter system to determine value of *I* 
		- values of *I* greater than 3 use the following letters after f in alphabetic order. g= 4, h = 5.

- **Magnetic quantum number**, usually give the symbol *m*, and is sometimes called the azimuthal quantum number, identifies the electrons angular momentum along some axis, called the axis of quantization and is often chosen to be the z axis. Electrons in states with higher *m* are traveling more perpendicular to the z axis than electrons in states with lower *m*.
	- Must be an integer
	- In the given state of an electron m must always lie between -*I* and *I*. so if  *n* = 2, *I* = 1, then *m* = -1, 0, +1. Another example is *n* = 2, *I* = 0, then *m* = 0.
	- Another way people explain *m* is by saying it determines the orientation of an electron orbital.
	- *m* only strongly influences the energy of an electron when the electron is orbiting in a **magnetic field**. That's because the orbital motion of an electron is a form of electric current, and all electric currents experience a force in the presence of a magnetic field. - **Spin quantum number**, usually give the symbol *s* or $m_s$, which tells you the state of the electrons spin
	- The value of *s* s always either $+1/2$ or $-1/2$ otherwise referred to as "up" and "down".
	- the spin state tells us about the direction of the electrons **spin magnetic dipole** with respect to some external magnetic field. In this case if the *s* is "up" it means the electrons spin magnetic dipole is aligned with the field, and a spin state of "down" means the electrons spin magnetic dipole is pointing in the opposite direction as the magnetic field.
	- In the presence of a magnetic field of strength B, the energy of an electron is changed by:
