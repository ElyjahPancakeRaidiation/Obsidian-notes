## **ACIDS**
- Acids typically have a hydrogen in front of their compound. Like HCl, HF, or $H C_(2)H_(3)O_(2)$
- If a hydrogen i attached to a nonmetal then typically its an acid. 
- Hydrogen with a positive charge $H^+$ is an acid. Usually
- Arrhenius definition is acids release $H^+$ ions into solutions
- Another definition is acids are proton donors
- pH that is less than 7 it is acidic

- Molecular substances that ionize in water
- Acids transfer $H^+$ ion to $H_2O$ to make $H_3O$ (aq)
	- $H^+$ ion = H atom without its $e^-$ = proton
- **Strong acids** ionize completely:
	- $H C l (a q) + H_(2)O (a q) - > H_(3)O^(+)(a q) + C l^(-)(a q)$
	- They will also produce strong electrolytes in water, the solution can conduct electricity
	- Strong Acids: HCI, HBr, Hl, $H N O_(3)$, $H C I O_(4)$, $H C I O_(3)$, $H_(2)S O_(4)$ (1st $H^+$ only?) All other acids are weak acids.
- **Weak acids** ionize in equilibrium:
	- $H F (a q) + H_(2)O (a q) < - > H_(3)O^(+)(a q) + F^(-)(a q)$
	- They will also produce weak electrolytes in water.
- usually the acids with more oxygen atoms are gonna be more acidic.-> only works with acids that have oxygen
- Acids react with active metals to produce $H_2$
- Acids can donate a number of $H^+$ ions but this will also equal the # of steps
	- Mono = one -> $H C l + H_(2)O - > H_(3)O^(+) + C l^(-)$ | **Mono-protic**
		- Gave 1 hydrogen to H2O
	- Di = two -> $H_(2)C O_(3) + H_(2)O < - > H_(3)O^(+) + H C O_(3)^(-)$ | **Di-protic**
		- However since the end result still has one more hydrogen we can donate another one making the equation -> $H C O_(3)^(-) + H_(2)O < - > H_(3)O^(+) + C O^(()2 -)_(3)$
		- This in turn produces 2 $H_3O^+$ 
	- Tri = three and is the same thing as three, produces three $H_3O^+$ and has three steps. If we had $H_(3)P O_(4)$ this will leave us with a conjugate base of $P O^(()3 -)_(4)$ | **Tri-protic**
	- However only the hydrogen at the beginning will be the acidic hydrogen that will be donated. $H C_(2)H_(3)O_(2)$ only 1 hydrogen will be taken, the one at the beginning, the $H_3$ will be left alone.
- **Binary acids**: (Aqueous solutions of binary hydrogen compounds)
	- Name = hydro-(stem of name of other element)-ic acid
	- usually only has one element next to the hydrogen
- **Oxo-acids**: stem of name of non-metal - suffix acid 
	- Hydrogen must be connected to a non metal and usually has two or more elements
- **Bronsted definition** explain proton ($H^+$) reactions that do no involve $H_2O$ : Any chemical reaction that transfers a $H^+$ to another can be viewed as a Bronsted acid-base reaction. The acid (proton donor) transfers a $H^+$ to a base (proton acceptor) like I said before.

## **BASES**
- Bases typically have a hydroxide atom like NaOH, KOH. 
- If a hydrogen is next to a metal like NaH it is a base. 
- Hydrogen with a positive charge $H^-$ is an base. Usually
- Arrhenius definition is bases release $O H^(-)$ ions into solutions.
- Another definition is acids are proton acceptors
-  pH that is greater than 7 it is basic
- **Strong bases** are soluble ionic compounds like KOH, NaOH, and $B a (O H)_(2)$ they ionize completely
	- Form strong electrolytes
- **Weak base** are insoluble ionic compounds like $A l (O H)^(3)$
	- Do not ionize completely and do not dissolve well in solutions(water).
- compounds with less hydrogen atoms are more basic then ones with more.


## **Oxidation-Reduction**
$F e_(2)O_(3)$  is iron (III) cation

- **Oxidation**: Loss of electrons; LEO
	- Reactant is being oxidized and is reducing the other reactant = reducing agent
- **Reduction**: Gain of electrons; GER
	- Being reduced and oxidizing the other reactant = oxidizing agent

- Electrons aren't lost or gained but transferred from one reactant to another.

#### **Oxidation numbers**

	- The oxidation of a pure element is always 0. Example Zn = 0, $O_2$ = 0, $F_2$ = 0, etc
	- The oxidation state of ions is the charge you see. $Z n^(()2 +)$  = +2.
